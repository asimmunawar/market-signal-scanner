# Small Long-Term Investor Playbook

`market-signal-scanner` is designed to help small long-term investors avoid common mistakes:

- chasing a ticker after a sharp move
- buying before writing a thesis
- ignoring sell-review signals
- confusing attention with durable fundamentals
- taking positions that are too large for the risk

The app is not a signal vending machine. It is a decision cockpit.

## Daily Workflow

1. Run a current scan.
2. Open Opportunity Map to see score versus risk.
3. Open Decision Guardrails before buying anything.
4. For any ticker that looks interesting, write the one-sentence thesis in the Decision Journal.
5. Use News Summary or Agent Research to validate the thesis with current sources.
6. If the setup is hot or crowded, wait, use a starter size, or skip.

## Building The Watchlist

Your watchlist should be broad enough to compare opportunities, but small enough that you can understand what you own.

Use Ticker Discovery when you want to research a theme in plain English:

- `top dividend companies`
- `water infrastructure`
- `companies providing water cooling technologies to data centers`
- `cybersecurity ETFs`
- `energy infrastructure`

Ticker Discovery returns U.S.-listed stocks and ETFs only. Treat the results as candidates to track, not recommendations to buy. After adding a ticker, run Scanner so it receives the same technical, risk, and fundamental review as everything else.

Remove tickers when:

- you no longer understand the business or fund
- the ticker was added for a short-lived narrative
- the asset is not relevant to your long-term process
- you are tracking too many names to review calmly

## Buy Discipline

Before buying, the investor should be able to answer:

- What do I believe this company, ETF, or crypto asset will do over years, not days?
- What is already priced in?
- What could break the thesis?
- Why am I buying now instead of waiting?
- How much can I lose emotionally and financially without making a bad follow-up decision?

The best candidate is not always the highest score. The better candidate is often:

- strong enough to deserve research
- not extremely overbought
- not dominated by volatility
- understandable
- position-sized modestly

## FOMO Guard

Decision Guardrails flags FOMO risk when recent returns, RSI, volume spikes, and volatility suggest the move may already be crowded.

When a ticker appears in FOMO Danger:

- do not buy just because it is moving
- wait for a pullback, consolidation, or fresh evidence
- write what would make the trade a mistake
- consider a tiny starter only if the long-term thesis is already researched

## Sell Review

Sell Review is not an automatic sell list. It is a list of tickers that deserve a thesis check.

Review exposure when:

- score falls below neutral
- recommendation turns Sell or Strong Sell
- price breaks materially below long-term moving averages
- drawdown becomes severe
- the original thesis no longer matches current evidence

The goal is to avoid two opposite mistakes:

- panic selling a good long-term asset during normal volatility
- holding a broken thesis because selling feels like admitting defeat

## Starter Sizing

For small accounts, the first position should usually be a starter, not a full commitment.

The Starter Position Planner gives rough allocation ideas from the latest scan. It intentionally pushes toward modest sizing because a small investor benefits from:

- learning before sizing up
- reducing regret
- keeping cash for better entries
- avoiding single-ticker overconfidence

## Research Standard

A ticker is research-ready only when the investor can connect:

- scanner score
- chart setup
- valuation
- fundamentals
- recent news
- risks
- personal time horizon

If the explanation is mostly “it is going up,” the answer is usually wait.

## What Each Evidence Type Means

Technical analysis asks: what are price, trend, momentum, volatility, and volume doing?

Fundamental analysis asks: is the business or ETF financially attractive, durable, and reasonably valued?

News analysis asks: what changed recently, how fresh are the sources, and does the story actually affect the long-term thesis?

None of these is enough alone. A healthy long-term decision usually needs all three to make sense together.

## Charts Without Guessing

Use Interactive Chart for live exploration: zoom, change duration, toggle indicators, and inspect support/resistance.

Use Static Chart Report when you want a saved Markdown explanation. The report explains:

- last price and scanner score
- RSI, volatility, and max drawdown
- nearby support and resistance
- moving-average trend signals
- positive and caution signals

Support/resistance and trendlines are heuristics. They are useful for context, not proof.
